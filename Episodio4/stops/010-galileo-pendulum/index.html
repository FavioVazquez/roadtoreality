Galileo's Pendulum

The clock hiding in a chandelier

1602 · Galileo Galilei

Galileo was sitting in the Pisa cathedral, bored during a long service, when he noticed a chandelier swinging in a draft. He timed it with his pulse and made a startling observation: no matter how wide or narrow the swing, it took the same amount of time.

The Physics

For small angles, the period of a pendulum is T = 2π√(L/g), where L is the length and g is gravitational acceleration. Notice what's missing from that formula: mass. Galileo's observation was correct — mass has no effect on the period whatsoever.

This property — isochronism — makes pendulums ideal for timekeeping. Christiaan Huygens built the first pendulum clock in 1656, and for two centuries, pendulum clocks were the most accurate timekeepers ever made. Navigation, astronomy, and the Industrial Revolution all depended on them.

The formula breaks down at large angles (above ~15°), where the period gets slightly longer. This is a real-world effect — try setting the angle to 80° and watching the period increase slightly compared to a 15° swing.

Key Takeaway
  • A pendulum's period depends only on its length: T = 2π√(L/g). Mass has no effect.
  • This property (isochronism) made pendulum clocks possible — the most precise timekeepers for two centuries.
  • The formula is approximate; at large angles the period increases slightly. Galileo noticed this too.

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$
