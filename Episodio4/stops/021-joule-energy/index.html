Joule and Conservation of Energy

Heat is just motion in disguise

1843 · James Prescott Joule

James Prescott Joule was a brewer who became one of the most precise experimentalists in history. He showed that mechanical work and heat are interchangeable — that they are both forms of the same thing, energy. His paddle-wheel experiment measured the conversion ratio with extraordinary accuracy, and in doing so established the first law of thermodynamics: energy is conserved across all its forms.

The Experiment

Joule suspended a known mass from a cord wound around a paddle-wheel axle. When the mass fell, it rotated the paddle through water. The temperature rise in the water was measured with extraordinary precision — a few thousandths of a degree per drop. By repeating the experiment thousands of times and accumulating the effect, Joule established that 4.184 joules of mechanical work raise one gram of water by one degree Celsius.

Why It Was Controversial

In 1843, most physicists believed in caloric — a weightless fluid that flowed from hot bodies to cold ones. Joule's result implied that heat is not a substance but a mode of motion. The temperature rise is tiny (drop a 2 kg weight from 1.5 m and you heat 1 kg of water by just 0.007°C), but it is perfectly reproducible — which is the whole point.

Key Concepts
  • Energy is conserved — it changes form, but the total never changes. Joule proved that mechanical work and heat are interchangeable.
  • $$E_{\text{total}} = mgh = \underbrace{mg(h-y)}_{\text{PE}} + \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}mv^2}_{\text{KE}} + \underbrace{Q}_{\text{heat}}$$
  • Joule's mechanical equivalent of heat: $1 \text{ cal} = 4.184 \text{ J}$
  • This result unified mechanics and thermodynamics, displacing the caloric theory of heat. It became the foundation of the first law of thermodynamics.
