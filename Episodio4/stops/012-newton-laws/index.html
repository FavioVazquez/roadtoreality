Newton's Laws of Motion

F = ma. Three laws that governed physics for 200 years.

1687 · Isaac Newton

In 1687, Newton published the Principia Mathematica — arguably the most important scientific book ever written. In it, he laid out three laws that describe how every object in the universe responds to forces.

Push the block below. Watch how force, mass, and acceleration relate. Notice that when you stop pushing, the block doesn't stop — it keeps moving (First Law). Heavier blocks accelerate less for the same force (Second Law). And the block pushes back against the wall with equal force (Third Law).

The Three Laws

First Law (Inertia): An object at rest stays at rest; an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted on by an external force. Before Newton, people thought things naturally "ran down." Newton said they keep going forever unless something stops them.

Second Law (F = ma): The acceleration of an object is proportional to the net force applied and inversely proportional to its mass. Double the force, double the acceleration. Double the mass, halve the acceleration.

Third Law (Action-Reaction): Every force has an equal and opposite reaction. When you push a wall, the wall pushes back with the same force. When a rocket expels gas backward, the gas pushes the rocket forward.

These three laws — combined with his law of gravitation — gave physics a unified mathematical framework that explained everything from falling apples to planetary orbits.

Key Takeaway
  • Objects don't need a force to keep moving — they need a force to change their motion (1st Law).
  • F = ma: acceleration is proportional to force and inversely proportional to mass (2nd Law).
  • Forces always come in equal and opposite pairs — you can never push without being pushed back (3rd Law).

$$F = ma$$

→ Newton's laws describe how forces affect motion. But what produces the force that holds the Moon in orbit and pulls apples from trees? That's his other great insight — universal gravitation.
