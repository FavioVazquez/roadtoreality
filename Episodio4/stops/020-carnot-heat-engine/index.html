Carnot and the Heat Engine

You can never convert all heat into work

1824 · Sadi Carnot

Carnot proved there is a fundamental limit to how efficiently any heat engine can run — a limit set by thermodynamics itself, not by engineering. What's happening

The four stages of the Carnot cycle are: (1) isothermal expansion at TH — the gas absorbs heat and expands; (2) adiabatic expansion — temperature drops from TH to TC; (3) isothermal compression at TC — the gas releases heat and is compressed; (4) adiabatic compression — temperature rises back to TH. The enclosed area equals net work done per cycle.

Key Concepts
  • The Carnot efficiency is the maximum possible efficiency for any heat engine operating between two temperatures. No real engine can exceed it.
  • $$\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}$$
  • The area enclosed by the PV cycle equals the net work done per cycle: \(W = Q_H - Q_C\).
  • To achieve 100% efficiency, the cold reservoir would need to be at absolute zero (0 K) — physically impossible.
  • Temperature must be in Kelvin for this formula. Celsius and Fahrenheit are shifts; ratios only make sense in Kelvin.
→ Carnot set the efficiency limit. Joule measured the mechanical equivalent of heat — establishing conservation of energy as a universal law that even Carnot cycles must obey.
