The Doppler Effect

Why an ambulance sounds different as it passes

1842 · Christian Doppler

In 1842 Christian Doppler noticed that stars emitting the same wavelength of light would appear bluer when approaching and redder when receding. The same mathematics applies to sound: when a source moves, the wavefronts it emits bunch up in front and spread apart behind. That bunching is a frequency shift — and it became the ruler astronomers use to measure the expansion of the universe.

The Doppler formula

When a source moves toward an observer at speed \(v_s\) and waves travel at \(v_w\), the observed frequency is:

$$f_\text{obs} = f_0 \cdot \frac{v_w}{v_w \mp v_s}$$

The minus sign applies when the source approaches (higher frequency); the plus sign when it recedes (lower frequency). For light and slow sources (\(v_s \ll c\)):

$$\frac{\Delta f}{f_0} \approx \frac{v_s}{c}$$

This redshift formula is how Edwin Hubble, using data from Vesto Slipher, discovered in 1929 that almost every galaxy is receding — and the further away it is, the faster it moves. The Doppler effect, worked out for sound in a Czech lecture hall, turned out to measure the size and fate of the cosmos.

Key Concept
  • A source moving toward you compresses its wavefronts — observed frequency rises. Moving away stretches them — frequency drops. The formula is \(f = f_0 \, v_w/(v_w \mp v_s)\). Redshift of distant galaxies is the Doppler effect applied to light.
→ Doppler connected motion to frequency. Boltzmann took a different approach — connecting temperature to the statistics of microscopic motion — and defined entropy as a count of possible arrangements.
