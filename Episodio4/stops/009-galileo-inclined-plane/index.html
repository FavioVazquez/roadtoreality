Galileo's Inclined Plane

How to measure gravity without a stopwatch

1604 · Galileo Galilei

The problem with studying free fall is that objects fall too fast to measure precisely with Renaissance instruments. Galileo's solution was elegant: tilt the plane. By rolling balls down an inclined surface instead of dropping them, he slowed gravity enough to time carefully.

He found that distance traveled grows as the square of elapsed time — always, regardless of the ball's mass. This is uniformly accelerated motion, and it contradicted Aristotle completely.

The Discovery

Galileo's inclined plane experiments led him to the law of uniform acceleration: distance = ½at². He used a water clock (measuring water flow as a proxy for time) and a grooved plank with bells at measured intervals. He could hear whether the ball hit each bell at equally spaced time intervals — confirming uniform acceleration.

The acceleration along the incline is g·sin(θ), where θ is the angle and g = 9.8 m/s². At 30° this is about 4.9 m/s². At 90° (free fall) it is the full 9.8 m/s². Crucially, at every angle, the result is identical for all masses — the 1 kg ball and the 10 kg ball reach the bottom at the same moment.

This was the birth of modern kinematics. Before Galileo, "natural philosophy" described motion in qualitative terms. After Galileo, physics measured it in equations.

Key Takeaway
  • On an inclined plane, acceleration = g·sin(θ) — independent of the object's mass.
  • Distance traveled grows as the square of elapsed time: d = ½at². This is Galileo's law of uniform acceleration.
  • Galileo invented the experimental method: use a controlled setup to isolate one variable at a time.

$$d = \frac{1}{2} a t^2$$

→ Galileo made another key discovery about motion from a different direction — looking up at a chandelier swinging in a cathedral draft. The pendulum would give him the first reliable clock.
