Euler and Rigid Body Rotation

Why gyroscopes don't fall over

~1750 · Leonhard Euler

Newton gave us F = ma for translating bodies. Euler extended this to rotation. A force applied off-center creates a torque, and that torque produces angular acceleration — but how much depends not on mass alone, but on how that mass is distributed around the axis of rotation.

The three shapes below — disk, rod, and ring — have identical mass and the same torque applied. Watch how their angular velocities diverge. The disk spins fastest; the ring, slowest. The graph traces every angular velocity over time, making the difference unmistakable.

Torque and angular acceleration

The rotational analogue of Newton's second law is:

$$\tau = I \alpha$$

where \(\tau\) is torque (N·m), \(I\) is moment of inertia (kg·m²), and \(\alpha\) is angular acceleration (rad/s²). The shape of the object determines \(I\).

Moments of inertia

For the three shapes in the simulation, each rotating about a central axis:

$$I_{\text{disk}} = \tfrac{1}{2}mr^2 \qquad I_{\text{rod}} = \tfrac{1}{3}mL^2 \qquad I_{\text{ring}} = mr^2$$

Given the same torque, the disk accelerates fastest (\(I\) is smallest), the ring slowest (\(I\) is largest). The angular velocity at time \(t\) is:

$$\omega(t) = \alpha \, t = \frac{\tau}{I} \, t$$

The graph on the right shows three diverging straight lines — one per shape — confirming that angular velocity rises linearly at a rate inversely proportional to \(I\).

Euler's equations for 3D rotation

In three dimensions Euler extended this to a set of equations relating the angular velocity components \(\omega_1, \omega_2, \omega_3\) along the principal axes of a body to the applied torques. These equations govern gyroscope precession, spacecraft attitude control, and the wobbly path of a thrown frisbee.

Key Equation
  • \(\tau = I\alpha\) — the rotational analogue of Newton's second law. A body with more mass spread far from the axis (high \(I\)) is harder to spin. This is why figure skaters pull in their arms to speed up, and why hollow wheels roll differently than solid ones.
