Faraday's Electromagnetic Induction

Move a magnet through a coil — electricity appears

1831 · Michael Faraday

Faraday — a bookbinder's apprentice with no formal education — discovered that a changing magnetic field creates an electric current. Move the magnet through the coil below and watch the galvanometer needle respond to the rate of change of flux, not to flux itself. Pull the magnet back out and the needle flips direction — Lenz's law in action.

What's happening

The galvanometer needle is driven by the time-derivative of magnetic flux through the coil — dΦ/dt — not by the flux itself. When the magnet sits still, no current flows. Only movement creates induction. And when the magnet reverses direction, the induced current reverses too (Lenz's law).

Key Concepts
  • A changing magnetic flux through a coil induces an electromotive force (EMF). The magnitude depends on how fast the flux changes — not on the flux itself.
  • $$\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$
  • Lenz's law: the induced current opposes the change that caused it. Move the magnet in → current one way; pull it out → current reverses.
  • $$\Phi_B = \int \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}$$
  • Every generator, transformer, and induction motor in the world runs on this principle.
→ Faraday gave us generators. Carnot asked a harder question: how much work can you extract from heat? His answer set a fundamental limit that no engineer has ever beaten — and never will.
