Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

Orbits are ellipses. Time moves unequally.

1609–1619 · Johannes Kepler

Kepler spent years staring at Tycho Brahe's precise observational data, trying to fit planets to perfect circles. Nothing worked — there was always a tiny error. Finally, in frustration, he tried an ellipse. It fit perfectly.

He then discovered something stranger: planets don't move at constant speed. They speed up near the Sun and slow down far away, but always sweep out the same area of space in the same amount of time. Watch the shaded triangle below — it stays constant even as the planet's speed changes.

The Three Laws

First Law (1609): Planets orbit the Sun in ellipses, with the Sun at one focus — not the center.

Second Law (1609): A line from the Sun to a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times. This means planets move faster when closer to the Sun (perihelion) and slower when farther away (aphelion).

Third Law (1619): The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of its average distance from the Sun: T² ∝ a³. This single equation links all planets in one mathematical relationship.

Kepler had no idea why these laws held. They were purely empirical — extracted from data through years of painstaking calculation. It took Newton, 70 years later, to show they all follow from a single law of gravitation.

Key Takeaway
  • Planets orbit in ellipses, not circles — the Sun is at one focus, not the center.
  • The equal-areas law (2nd law) means planets speed up near the Sun and slow down far away.
  • Kepler found these patterns purely empirically. Newton later explained them with gravitation.
