Archimedes and Buoyancy

Eureka! Why things float

~250 BCE · Archimedes of Syracuse

The king of Syracuse had a problem: was his gold crown really gold, or had the craftsman mixed in cheaper silver? He asked Archimedes to find out without melting the crown. The answer came in the bath.

Archimedes noticed that his body displaced water when he got in. He realized the volume of water displaced equals the volume of the submerged object — and that the water pushes back with a force equal to its own weight. This is the buoyant force.

The Principle

Archimedes' principle states: an object submerged in fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces. If that force is greater than the object's weight, the object floats. If less, it sinks.

This is why a massive steel ship floats — its hollow interior displaces far more water than its hull weighs. And why a solid steel ball sinks — it displaces less water than its weight requires.

Archimedes reportedly ran naked through the streets of Syracuse shouting "Eureka!" (I have found it!) when he made the connection. He then used it to prove the crown had been adulterated — the crown displaced more water than an equal weight of pure gold would have, revealing its lower density.

Key Takeaway
  • Buoyant force = weight of displaced fluid. If buoyancy exceeds gravity, the object floats.
  • What matters is density (mass per volume), not mass alone. A hollow steel ship floats; a solid steel ball sinks.
  • Archimedes' insight was the first quantitative principle in fluid mechanics — 2,000 years before Newton.
