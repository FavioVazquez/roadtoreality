Thales and Natural Causes

What if the universe runs on rules, not gods?

~600 BCE · Thales of Miletus

Before Thales, the Greeks explained earthquakes as Poseidon shaking the ground, lightning as Zeus throwing bolts, and eclipses as divine omens. These explanations worked — they gave frightened people a story — but they made no predictions you could test.

Thales made one move that changed everything: he asked whether nature has patterns that repeat regardless of what any god wants. He proposed that earthquakes happen because the Earth floats on water, and water disturbs it. He was wrong about the mechanism. But the question itself — "what natural process causes this?" — is the founding question of all science.

Why This Moment Matters

Thales lived in Miletus, a trading city on the coast of present-day Turkey. He had contact with Babylonian astronomers and Egyptian geometers — people who had already noticed that the sky follows patterns. Thales absorbed this and drew the radical conclusion: if the sky has patterns, so does everything else.

He predicted a solar eclipse in 585 BCE — a prediction so startling that when it came true, it ended a war. Two armies were so shaken by the eclipse they had been watching in the middle of a battle that they signed a peace treaty on the spot.

His specific theories about what things are made of (he thought water was the fundamental substance) turned out to be wrong. But the method — look for a natural cause, form a prediction, check it against reality — became the engine of 2,500 years of discovery.

Key Takeaway
  • Thales was the first recorded thinker to demand natural (not supernatural) explanations for natural events — the birth of scientific thinking.
  • He was wrong about the specifics (water as the fundamental substance) but right about the approach: nature has patterns that repeat and can be predicted.
  • His eclipse prediction in 585 BCE was the first recorded scientific forecast — and it worked.
