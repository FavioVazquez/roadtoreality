Eratosthenes Measures the Earth

A stick, a shadow, and the size of a world

~240 BCE · Eratosthenes of Cyrene

Eratosthenes was the chief librarian of Alexandria. He read in a scroll that in the city of Syene (modern Aswan), on the summer solstice, the sun shone directly down a deep well — no shadow at all. In Alexandria, 800 km north, a vertical stick always cast a shadow on that day.

He realized this could only happen on a curved surface. By measuring the angle of Alexandria's shadow and knowing the distance between the cities, he calculated Earth's circumference. His answer: about 40,000 km. The modern value is 40,075 km.

The Geometry

The key insight: if the Earth is spherical, parallel rays of sunlight hit different latitudes at different angles. In Syene (on the Tropic of Cancer at solstice), the sun is directly overhead — angle 0°. In Alexandria, the same sunlight hits at an angle of about 7.2°.

That 7.2° is 1/50th of a full 360° circle. So if the distance between the cities (800 km) equals 1/50th of the circumference, the full circumference must be 50 × 800 = 40,000 km. That's all the mathematics required.

Eratosthenes lived before calculators, telescopes, or any precision instruments beyond a stick and a measuring cord. His result remained the best estimate of Earth's size for over a thousand years.

Key Takeaway
  • Eratosthenes used the shadow angle difference between two cities to measure Earth's circumference — and got within 2% of the correct answer.
  • The only inputs he needed: the shadow angle at one city, and the distance between the two cities.
  • This is one of the most elegant experiments in history: profound result from the simplest possible observation.
