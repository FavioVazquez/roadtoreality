Maxwell's Equations

Four equations that contain all of classical electromagnetism

1865 · James Clerk Maxwell

James Clerk Maxwell looked at all of the known laws of electricity and magnetism — Gauss, Faraday, Ampère — and realized they were inconsistent. By adding one term (the "displacement current") he made them consistent, and in doing so predicted that oscillating electric and magnetic fields would sustain each other in a self-propagating wave. He calculated the speed: exactly the measured speed of light. Light is an electromagnetic wave.

Maxwell's Four Equations
  • Gauss's Law (electric): electric field lines originate on positive charges and terminate on negative charges.
  • $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}$$
  • Gauss's Law (magnetic): there are no magnetic monopoles — field lines always form closed loops.
  • $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$$
  • Faraday's Law: a changing magnetic field creates an electric field.
  • $$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$
  • Ampère-Maxwell Law: a changing electric field (or current) creates a magnetic field.
  • $$\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$$
  • Together, these four equations predict that oscillating electric and magnetic fields sustain each other — forming a self-propagating wave at speed \(c = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}} \approx 3 \times 10^8\) m/s.
→ Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism into light. Doppler showed that the motion of a source shifts the frequency of the waves it emits — a tool that would later reveal the expansion of the universe itself.
