Bernoulli's Principle

Why airplanes fly and curveballs curve

1738 · Daniel Bernoulli

In 1738, Daniel Bernoulli published Hydrodynamica and made a startling observation: when a fluid is forced through a narrow section of pipe, it speeds up — and as it speeds up, its pressure drops. The two effects are inseparable. Energy is conserved, but it trades between kinetic and pressure form.

The continuity equation

Before Bernoulli, fluids already obeyed one simple rule: what flows in must flow out. For an incompressible fluid, the product of cross-sectional area and velocity is constant:

$$A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2$$

A narrower throat means a higher velocity. That is the continuity equation — purely geometric, no energy required.

Where pressure enters

Bernoulli's insight was to apply conservation of energy to a fluid element moving along a streamline. The total energy per unit volume — pressure energy, kinetic energy, and gravitational potential — stays constant:

$$P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{const}$$

At constant height this simplifies to \(P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 = \text{const}\). If \(v\) rises at the throat (continuity demands it), \(P\) must fall by exactly \(\tfrac{1}{2}\rho(v_2^2 - v_1^2)\). That pressure difference is what holds aircraft aloft.

Real applications

Airfoil lift, the curveball's break, carburetors, atomizers, venturi flowmeters — all follow the same exchange. Faster fluid, lower pressure. The principle is deceptively simple and extraordinarily useful.

Key Equation
  • \(P + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 = \text{const}\) — along a streamline at constant height. Where fluid speeds up, pressure drops by \(\Delta P = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(v_2^2 - v_1^2)\). This pressure difference is what lifts every aircraft in the sky.
