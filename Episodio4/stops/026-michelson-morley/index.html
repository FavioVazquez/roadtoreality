The Michelson-Morley Experiment

The most famous null result in physics

1887 · Michelson & Morley

In 1887, Albert Michelson and Edward Morley built the most sensitive interferometer ever made and set it spinning on a stone slab floating in mercury. They expected to detect Earth's motion through the hypothetical luminiferous ether. Instead, they found nothing — the most important null result in the history of physics.

The interferometer splits a beam of light and sends the two halves along perpendicular arms. When they recombine at the beam splitter, they interfere — producing bright and dark fringes that are exquisitely sensitive to any difference in travel time.

Classical physics predicted that if Earth moves through a stationary ether at speed $v_E$, the two beams would take different times to travel and return, because one arm points partly into the ether wind. Rotating the apparatus by 90° should swap the pattern and shift the fringes by a measurable amount.

Michelson and Morley observed no fringe shift at all — not just a small shift, but essentially zero — far below what any ether theory could explain away.

Key Concepts
  • Michelson and Morley expected Earth's motion through the hypothetical luminiferous ether to produce a measurable fringe shift when rotating the interferometer. They found nothing.
  • The expected fringe shift from an aether wind of speed $v_E$ would be:
  • $$\Delta\phi_{\text{expected}} = \frac{4\pi L v_E^2}{\lambda c^2}$$
  • The observed fringe shift was consistent with zero — the null result. The speed of light is the same in all directions, regardless of Earth's motion.
  • $$\Delta\phi_{\text{observed}} \approx 0$$
  • This result could not be explained by classical physics. It pointed directly toward Einstein's 1905 postulate: the speed of light is constant in all inertial frames.
→ The ether was gone. Classical physics was in crisis. Planck's desperate solution to the blackbody problem introduced an entirely new idea: energy comes in discrete chunks.
