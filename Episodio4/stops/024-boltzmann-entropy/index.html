Boltzmann and Entropy

Why the universe always gets messier

1877 · Ludwig Boltzmann

In 1877, Ludwig Boltzmann gave entropy a precise definition: it counts the number of microscopic arrangements (microstates) \(\Omega\) that produce the same macroscopic appearance. Because high-entropy states have astronomically more microstates than low-entropy ones, a system evolving randomly will almost certainly move toward disorder — not because nature "wants" disorder, but because there are so many more ways to be disordered. His formula is carved on his gravestone in Vienna.

The Maxwell-Boltzmann speed distribution

In a gas at temperature \(T\), particle speeds are not all equal — they follow a statistical distribution. In two dimensions, the probability density is:

$$f(v) = \frac{m}{k_B T}\, v\, \exp\!\left(-\frac{mv^2}{2k_B T}\right)$$

The peak shifts to higher speeds as temperature rises, and the distribution broadens. The yellow curve in the left panel shows this theoretical prediction.

Boltzmann entropy

Entropy \(S\) is proportional to the logarithm of the number of microstates \(\Omega\):

$$S = k_B \ln \Omega$$

When the right-panel partition is removed, the gas expands into twice the volume. The number of accessible microstates doubles for each particle, so \(\Omega \to 2^N \Omega_0\) and entropy increases by \(\Delta S = N k_B \ln 2\). The entropy meter tracks the fraction of available phase space the gas has explored after the wall opens.

Key Concept
  • Boltzmann's equation \(S = k_B \ln \Omega\) links the macroscopic concept of entropy to the count of microscopic arrangements. The second law of thermodynamics — entropy never decreases — is really a statement about probability: disordered states are overwhelmingly more likely than ordered ones.
  • The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution \(f(v) \propto v \exp(-mv^2/2k_BT)\) describes how particle speeds are distributed in thermal equilibrium at temperature \(T\).
