The Ultraviolet Catastrophe

Classical physics, as captured by the Rayleigh-Jeans law, predicted that a hot object should emit infinite energy at high frequencies. Heat up any oven and you should instantly be blasted with deadly X-rays and gamma rays. Obviously this never happens — but the equations insisted it should. Physicists called this absurd prediction the ultraviolet catastrophe.

Planck's Desperate Act

In 1900, Max Planck found the correct formula by assuming that energy could only be emitted in discrete packets proportional to frequency: E = hf, where h is now called Planck's constant. Planck himself described this assumption as "a desperate act" — a mathematical trick to fix the equations, not a physical claim he believed. It took Einstein to take the idea seriously.

Wien's Displacement Law

The peak of the blackbody spectrum shifts to higher frequencies (shorter wavelengths) as temperature increases. Hot stars are blue-white; cooler stars are red. Our Sun peaks in the yellow-green. You can see this in the simulation: drag the temperature slider and watch the peak move.

Key Equations

$$E = hf$$

$$B(\nu, T) = \frac{2h\nu^3}{c^2} \cdot \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/k_BT} - 1}$$

When \(h \to 0\), Planck's formula reduces to the classical Rayleigh-Jeans law \(B \propto \nu^2 T\). Drag the h slider to the left to see both curves converge.

→ Planck quantized energy as a mathematical trick. Einstein took it literally and used it to explain the photoelectric effect — proving that light itself comes in packets we now call photons.
