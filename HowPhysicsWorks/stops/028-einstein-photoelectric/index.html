Einstein and the Photoelectric Effect

Light is both a wave and a particle

1905 · Albert Einstein

Einstein explained why shining light on metal ejects electrons — but only if the light frequency is high enough. This won him the Nobel Prize and proved light comes in photons.

The Classical Prediction (Wrong)

Classical wave theory predicted that if you shone bright enough light on metal, eventually enough energy would accumulate to eject electrons. The brighter the light, the more energy deposited — so crank up the intensity long enough and electrons would eventually escape. Try it: drag the intensity slider to maximum while the frequency slider is below the threshold. Nothing happens. No electrons, no matter how bright the light.

Einstein's Insight: Photons

In 1905 Einstein proposed that light comes in discrete packets — photons — each carrying energy E = hf. Only a photon with enough frequency (enough energy per packet) can knock an electron loose. More photons means more intensity, but each photon still carries the same energy. Intensity controls the rate of ejection; frequency controls whether ejection is possible at all.

The Work Function

Each metal has a characteristic work function φ — the minimum energy needed to free an electron from the surface. Any energy beyond φ becomes kinetic energy of the ejected electron: KE = hf − φ. This linear relationship between KE and frequency (not intensity) was Einstein's key prediction, verified experimentally by Millikan in 1916.

Key Equation

$$KE = hf - \varphi$$

Above the threshold frequency \(f_0 = \varphi/h\), kinetic energy increases linearly with \(f\), not with intensity.

→ Light is a particle. And a wave. Einstein went further: if the speed of light is constant for all observers, space and time themselves must be relative — moving clocks run slow.
