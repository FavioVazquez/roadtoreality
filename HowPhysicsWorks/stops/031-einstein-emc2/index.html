Mass Is Frozen Energy

A single gram of matter — a paperclip — contains as much energy as a 21-kiloton nuclear bomb. The conversion factor is c², and c² is enormous: about 9 × 10¹⁶ joules per kilogram. This is why nuclear reactions release so much energy: even tiny amounts of mass convert to colossal amounts of energy.

The Complete Equation

E = mc² is famous, but it is actually a special case. The full equation of Special Relativity is E² = (pc)² + (mc²)², where p is relativistic momentum p = γmv. When an object is at rest, p = 0, and the equation reduces to E = mc². When moving, the total energy is larger — the extra part is kinetic energy.

What the Velocity Slider Shows

The lower slider lets you give your mass a velocity. Watch how the total energy grows: the rest energy (mc²) stays fixed, while relativistic kinetic energy (γ−1)mc² adds on top. As v→c, kinetic energy grows without bound — which is why you can never accelerate a massive object to the speed of light.

Key Equations

$$E = mc^2$$

But the complete relativistic energy equation is:

$$E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$$

where $p = \gamma m v$ is relativistic momentum. $E = mc^2$ is the special case $p = 0$ — an object at rest.
