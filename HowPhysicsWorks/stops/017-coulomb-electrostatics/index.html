Coulomb's Law

Electricity follows an inverse square law — just like gravity

1785 · Charles-Augustin de Coulomb

In 1785, Charles-Augustin de Coulomb used a delicate torsion balance — a wire that twists under tiny forces — to measure the repulsion between two charged spheres with extraordinary precision. He found something striking: the electric force between charges obeys exactly the same mathematical form as Newton's law of gravity. Both forces follow an inverse-square law. But electricity adds something gravity cannot do — it can repel as well as attract.

What Coulomb discovered

Before Coulomb, the qualitative fact that like charges repel and opposite charges attract was well known. What was missing was the precise mathematical law. Coulomb's torsion balance let him measure the restoring torque with enough precision to establish that the force between two charges scales as:

$$F = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$

where \(k = 8.99 \times 10^9\) N·m²/C² is Coulomb's constant, \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) are the charges (positive or negative), and \(r\) is the separation. When the charges have the same sign, \(q_1 q_2 > 0\) and the force is repulsive. Opposite signs give attraction.

The parallel with Newton's gravity

The structural resemblance to Newton's gravitational law \(F = Gm_1m_2/r^2\) is not a coincidence — both arise from the same mathematical requirement that a central force acting radially in three-dimensional space must fall off as \(1/r^2\). The key difference: gravity only attracts, while the electric force can do both. And the electric force between two protons is about \(10^{36}\) times stronger than their gravitational attraction — electromagnetic forces dominate all of chemistry, biology, and materials science.

Real-world anchors

Coulomb's law is responsible for the cling of freshly washed clothing, the snapping of a spark from a doorknob after crossing a carpet, and the behavior of every protein and DNA strand in your body. Every chemical bond — ionic, covalent, hydrogen — is a consequence of the inverse-square law between charged particles at atomic separations.

Key Concept
  • The electric force between two charges follows \(F = kq_1q_2/r^2\). Same sign: repulsion. Opposite sign: attraction. Halve the distance: force quadruples. This single law — combined with Newton's — governs all of electrostatics.
  • Coulomb's constant \(k \approx 9 \times 10^9\) N·m²/C² makes the electric force between charges of 1 C separated by 1 m equal to 9 billion newtons — far stronger than gravity at any atomic scale.
→ Coulomb showed that static charges exert forces governed by an inverse-square law. Volta asked: what if you could sustain those forces as a continuous flow of charge? His answer was the battery — the first device capable of driving a steady electric current, and the key that unlocked all of electrical engineering.
