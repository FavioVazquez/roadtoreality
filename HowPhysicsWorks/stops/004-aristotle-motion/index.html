Aristotle's Theory of Motion

Why heavy things fall faster (they don't)

~350 BCE · Aristotle

Aristotle taught that heavier objects fall faster than lighter ones — proportionally. A 10 kg stone should fall 10 times faster than a 1 kg stone. This seemed obvious. Rocks fall, feathers float. For 1,800 years, no one seriously challenged it.

What Aristotle Believed

Aristotle's theory was part of a larger framework: everything has a "natural place." Earth and water belong below, fire and air above. Heavy objects (more earth) fall faster because they are "seeking" their natural place more urgently. Light objects (more air) rise. It was internally consistent and matched casual observation.

Galileo is credited with disproving this — possibly by dropping balls from the Leaning Tower of Pisa, though historians debate whether that demonstration actually happened. What he definitely did was roll balls down inclined planes and measure times precisely. The result: all objects accelerate at the same rate regardless of mass (ignoring air resistance).

The only case where Aristotle's intuition seems right is when air resistance is significant — a feather falls slowly not because it's light, but because its large surface area creates more drag relative to its weight. With air resistance off, mass doesn't matter at all.

Key Takeaway
  • All objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass — in the absence of air resistance.
  • Aristotle's theory felt obvious because air resistance is real and everyday objects experience it differently.
  • This error persisted for 1,800 years because no one did a careful, controlled experiment to check it.

Aristotle believed:

$$v \propto \frac{F}{m}$$

Newton corrected this to:

$$a = \frac{F}{m}$$

→ While Aristotle was wrong about falling objects, he was at least asking good questions about motion. A century later, Archimedes tackled a different kind of motion — things moving through water — and got it exactly right.
