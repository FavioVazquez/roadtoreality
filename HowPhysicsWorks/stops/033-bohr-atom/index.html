Bohr's Model of the Atom

Electrons jump between fixed energy levels

The Classical Prediction: Catastrophe

An electron orbiting a nucleus is an accelerating charge. Accelerating charges radiate energy (Stop 025 — Hertz). As the electron radiates, it loses energy and spirals inward, eventually crashing into the nucleus in less than a nanosecond. Classical physics predicts every atom should instantly collapse. Watch the simulation — it does.

Bohr's Radical Proposal

In 1913, Niels Bohr simply declared that electrons in atoms do not obey classical radiation laws. They can only occupy specific quantized orbits, each corresponding to a discrete energy level. An electron in a Bohr orbit does not radiate. The atom is stable by decree.

The Emission Spectrum as Proof

When an electron jumps from a higher orbit to a lower one, it emits a photon with exactly the energy difference. The photon's frequency — and therefore its color — is determined by which two levels are involved. Each element has a unique set of allowed orbits, producing a unique spectral fingerprint. Hydrogen's Balmer series (n→2 transitions) falls in the visible range.

Key Equations

$$E_n = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}$$

$$\Delta E = h\nu = 13.6\left(\frac{1}{n_{\text{low}}^2} - \frac{1}{n_{\text{high}}^2}\right) \text{ eV}$$

The Balmer series ($n \to 2$): H$\alpha$ = 656 nm (red), H$\beta$ = 486 nm (cyan), H$\gamma$ = 434 nm (violet).
