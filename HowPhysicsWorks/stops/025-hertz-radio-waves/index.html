Hertz and Radio Waves

Maxwell's invisible light, detected at last

1887 · Heinrich Hertz

In 1887, Heinrich Hertz built a spark-gap transmitter and a loop detector just a few meters away. When sparks jumped in the transmitter, sparks also jumped in the detector — confirming that invisible electromagnetic waves travel through space at the speed of light, exactly as Maxwell had predicted 22 years earlier.

A dipole antenna — two conducting rods with alternating charge — is the simplest EM radiator. The radiation is not uniform in all directions: it is zero directly along the antenna axis and strongest perpendicular to it. This angular dependence is one of the most fundamental results in electrodynamics.

Each half-cycle of oscillation reverses the E-field direction, producing a new wavefront of opposite polarity. The colored rings in the simulation show successive half-cycles: red for positive E-field, blue for negative. The spacing between rings equals the wavelength λ = v/f.

Key Concepts
  • Hertz proved that oscillating electric charges radiate electromagnetic waves — exactly as Maxwell's equations predicted. The radiation pattern is not spherical: it is zero along the dipole axis and maximum perpendicular to it.
  • $$I(\theta) \propto \sin^2\!\theta$$
  • The emitted wave travels at the speed of light. Each half-cycle reverses the E-field direction — the alternating red and blue rings represent successive half-cycles.
  • $$c = \lambda f = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}}$$
  • Hertz's experiment — done in 1887 — directly confirmed Maxwell's 1865 prediction and opened the door to radio, television, and wireless communication.
