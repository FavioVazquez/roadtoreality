Rutherford and the Atomic Nucleus

The atom is mostly empty space

1911 · Ernest Rutherford

Rutherford fired alpha particles at gold foil. Most passed straight through — but a few bounced directly back. The atom has a tiny, dense nucleus at its center.

The Plum Pudding Model (Wrong)

In 1909, the prevailing model of the atom — Thomson's "plum pudding" — had positive charge spread uniformly throughout the atom, with electrons embedded like raisins in dough. Under this model, alpha particles should barely deflect when passing through gold foil. The positive charge would be too diffuse to cause significant scattering.

The Unexpected Result

Most alpha particles did pass straight through — confirming the atom is mostly empty space. But about 1 in 8000 scattered at angles greater than 90 degrees. Rutherford said this was "as if you fired a 15-inch shell at tissue paper and it came back and hit you." No diffuse charge distribution could cause that.

The Nuclear Model

The only way to explain the backscattering was if all the positive charge — and nearly all the mass — was concentrated in an impossibly tiny region at the center. Rutherford called it the nucleus. It is 100,000 times smaller than the atom itself. The electrons orbit this nucleus in the vast empty space around it.

Rutherford Scattering Formula

$$\cot\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \frac{2b \, E_k}{k Z_1 Z_2 e^2}$$

Larger $b$ (impact parameter) = smaller deflection angle $\theta$. A head-on collision ($b = 0$) gives backscatter ($\theta = 180°$).

→ The nucleus was discovered. But electrons could not orbit it stably according to classical physics. Bohr fixed this by quantizing the electron's allowed orbits.
